Tinapa History

Smoking has been a traditional preservation method since pre-colonial times, ensuring fish remained edible for longer periods. Pre-colonial Filipinos used open-fire smoking methods for bangus and galunggong, enhancing their flavor while making them resistant to spoilage. Tinapa remains a breakfast favorite today, often served with rice and vinegar dipping sauce or used as an ingredient in other dishes like pancit and fried rice. The long history of smoking methods shows how early Filipinos developed ways to preserve their seafood, ensuring it remained a reliable food source even before modern storage techniques. /p>

Tinapa Ingredients

  • Galunggong
  • Salt
  • Water
  • Hickory Wood

Tinapa Recipe

  • 5 Lbs of Galunggong fish
  • 1 Quart of Salt
  • 3 Quarts of water
  • 2 Lbs. of Hickory wood chunks for smoking

Tinapa Procedure

    Notes

    Soak wood chunks in water two days before using it.

    For The Brine

  1. In a large bowl or small bucket add warm water and dissolve the salt.
  2. Clean the fish and add the brine onto the fish.
  3. I just use our kitchen sink for this (make sure your sink is clean and rinsed thoroughly).
  4. Let it brine for 1 hour while stirring the brine every ten minutes.
  5. The rule of thumb: brine the fish for ½ an hour for every half inch (thickness) of fish.
  6. After an hour remove the fish from the brine, rinse it well, and set aside.

    7. The Smoker

  7. Depending on your smoker, the best way to do this is to keep the fish away from the heat source as much as possible.
  8. Place your fish on the rack and add your wood chunks to the heat source.
  9. Cover the smoker and let it smoke for 1 ½ hour.
  10. You will need to add wood chunks every 20 minutes to keep the smoke going.
  11. Enjoy your freshly made Tinapa.
  12. Serve with fresh tomatoes and onions and don’t forget the garlic flavored vinegar dipping sauce.
