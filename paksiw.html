Paksiw a Bangus History

Paksiw na bangus is a classic dish where milkfish is simmered in vinegar, garlic, ginger, and spices, following an ancient preservation method used by early Filipinos. The term "paksiw" refers to the souring technique, originally applied to meats and fish to extend shelf life before refrigeration existed. Bangus, or milkfish, is considered the Philippine national fish, and due to its abundance, it has been widely used in various traditional recipes. The sour vinegar base helps kill bacteria while enhancing the flavor of the fish, making paksiw a favorite household dish for its longevity and simplicity. Over time, influences from Spanish colonizers introduced garlic, onions, and chili peppers into Filipino cuisine, refining the traditional paksiw recipe. Today, paksiw na bangus remains a popular everyday dish, often paired with rice and sometimes modified with added vegetables or miso to create deeper flavors.

Paksiw a Bangus Recipe

  • 1 large milkfish
  • 1 large eggplant, cut into 1-inch thick wedges
  • 1 onion, peeled and sliced thinly
  • 4 garlic, peeled and pounded
  • 1 thumb-size ginger, sliced into thirds and pounded
  • ½ cup vinegar
  • 1 cup water
  • 3 finger chili peppers
  • 1 ½ teaspoons salt
  • 1 teaspoon peppercorns
  • 1 teaspoon canola oil

Paksiw a Bangus Procedure

  1. Clean and gut the fish, leaving scales intact. Cut into serving parts.
  2. In a wide pot combine fish, eggplant, onion, garlic, ginger, vinegar, water, finger chili peppers, salt, and peppercorns.
  3. Cover and let simmer for about 10 to 15 minutes or until fish is cooked and liquid is reduced.
  4. Drizzle oil on top and stir gently to combine. Serve hot.
