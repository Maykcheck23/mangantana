Sinigang History

Sinigang is one of the most iconic and beloved Filipino sour soups, deeply rooted in indigenous Filipino culinary traditions, utilizing souring agents readily available in the archipelago. While tamarind is the most common souring agent, various fruits like calamansi, green mango, kamias, or guava can also be used. Sinigang Baka specifically uses beef as its main protein, often beef ribs or shanks, adding a rich depth to the tangy broth. It's a staple in Filipino households, celebrated for its comforting, refreshing, and distinctly sour flavor, perfect for any meal.

Sinigang Ingredients

  • Beef Ribs or Beef Shanks
  • Tomato
  • Onion
  • Radish
  • Long Beans
  • Eggplant
  • Okra
  • Green Chilies
  • Tamarind Juice or Powder

Sinigang Recipe

  • 1 kg beef ribs or beef shank, cut into serving pieces
  • 2-3 large ripe tomatoes, quartered
  • 1 large onion, quartered
  • 1 large radish (labanos), sliced
  • 1 bundle long beans (sitaw), cut into 2-inch pieces
  • 1 large eggplant, sliced
  • 4-5 pieces okra, trimmed
  • 2-3 green chilies (siling haba),
  • Tamarind Juice or Powder

Sinigang Procedure

  1. Tenderize Beef: In a large pot, combine beef ribs/shank and water. Bring to a boil, skim off any scum that rises to the surface. Reduce heat to low, cover, and simmer for 1.5 to 2.5 hours, or until beef is very tender.
  2. Add Souring Agents and Aromatics: Add tomatoes, onions, and green chilies (if using) to the pot. If using fresh tamarind, boil the tamarind with a little water until soft, then mash and strain to extract the pulp. Add the tamarind pulp or the sinigang mix to the broth.
  3. Add Vegetables: Once the beef is tender and the desired sourness is achieved, add the radish and long beans. Simmer for 5-7 minutes.
  4. Add Remaining Vegetables: Add eggplant and okra. Cook for another 3-5 minutes, or until all vegetables are tender-crisp.
  5. Season and Serve: Season with fish sauce to taste. Taste and adjust sourness or saltiness as needed. Serve hot with steamed rice
