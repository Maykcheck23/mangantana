Kare-kare History

Kare-Kare is one of the most distinctive and beloved Filipino stews, characterized by its thick, savory peanut sauce. Its origins are often debated, with theories pointing to Indian or even Muslim Filipino influences (due to the use of peanuts in some regional cuisines). The dish typically uses oxtail, tripe, and other beef cuts, along with various vegetables. The signature orange hue comes from annatto seeds (atsuete). Kare-Kare is a celebratory dish, often served at fiestas and family gatherings, requiring patience due to the long cooking time for the meat to become tender.

Kare-kare Recipe

  • 1 kg oxtail, cut into 2-inch pieces (or a combination of oxtail, beef tripe, beef stewing cuts)
  • 1 large onion, chopped
  • 4-5 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 cup peanut butter (unsweetened)
  • 1/2 cup toasted ground rice (or 2-3 tablespoons rice flour dissolved in water)
  • 1/4 cup annatto seeds (atsuete) soaked in 1/2 cup hot water (or 1 teaspoon annatto powder)
  • 1 bundle bok choy or pechay, separated
  • 1 bundle long beans (sitaw), cut into 2-inch pieces
  • 1 large eggplant, sliced
  • 1 banana blossom (puso ng saging), trimmed and sliced
  • 8-10 cups water or beef broth
  • 2 tablespoons cooking oil
  • Salt to taste
  • Bagoong alamang (fermented shrimp paste) for serving

Kare-kare Procedure

  1. Tenderize Meat: In a large pot or pressure cooker, combine oxtail (and tripe, if using) with water or beef broth. Bring to a boil, skim off impurities, then reduce heat to low, cover, and simmer for 2-3 hours (or 45-60 minutes in a pressure cooker) until the meat is very tender. Reserve the broth.
  2. Prepare Annatto Oil: If using annatto seeds, soak them in hot water for 10-15 minutes, then mash to extract the color. Strain to get annatto liquid.
  3. Sauté Aromatics: In a separate large pot or Dutch oven, heat cooking oil over medium heat. Sauté minced garlic and chopped onions until fragrant.
  4. Make Peanut Sauce: Add the tenderized meat to the pot. Pour in the reserved beef broth and annatto liquid (or powder). Bring to a simmer. Stir in the peanut butter until fully dissolved and smooth. Add the toasted ground rice (or dissolved rice flour) and stir continuously until the sauce thickens.
  5. Cook Vegetables: Add the banana blossom, long beans, and eggplant. Cook until vegetables are tender-crisp. Lastly, add the bok choy or pechay and cook for just a few minutes until wilted.
  6. Season and Serve: Season with salt to taste. Serve hot with a generous side of bagoong alamang.
