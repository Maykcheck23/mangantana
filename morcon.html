Morcon History

Beef Morcon is a festive and elaborate Filipino dish, a roulade or stuffed beef roll, clearly inspired by Spanish culinary traditions. Its name is derived from a Spanish sausage, though the Filipino version is a cooked dish, not a cured meat. It is a testament to the Spanish influence on Filipino cuisine, particularly in the preparation of special occasion dishes. Morcon is typically served during holidays like Christmas, New Year, and other significant family gatherings, showcasing both culinary skill and generosity.

Morcon Recipe

  • 1-1.5 kg beef flank steak or top round, butterflied or thinly sliced into a large rectangular piece
  • 1/2 cup hard-boiled eggs, quartered
  • 1/2 cup chorizos de Bilbao or regular hotdogs, sliced into sticks
  • 1/2 cup pickles (gherkins), sliced into sticks
  • 1/2 cup cheese, cut into sticks
  • 1/4 cup carrots, cut into sticks
  • 1/4 cup green bell pepper, cut into sticks
  • 1 can (8 oz) tomato sauce
  • 1/2 cup beef broth
  • 1/4 cup soy sauce
  • 1/4 cup vinegar
  • 1 large onion, chopped
  • 4-5 cloves garlic, minced
  • 2-3 bay leaves
  • 1 teaspoon black peppercorns
  • Cooking twine or skewers
  • 2-3 tablespoons cooking oil
  • Salt and black pepper to taste

Morcon Procedure

  1. Prepare Beef: Lay the butterflied beef flat on a clean surface. Season lightly with salt and pepper.
  2. Stuff Beef: Arrange the stuffing ingredients (eggs, chorizos, pickles, cheese, carrots, bell pepper) in rows along one edge of the beef.
  3. Roll and Secure: Tightly roll the beef over the stuffing, creating a firm log. Secure the roll with cooking twine or skewers at intervals.
  4. Brown Morcon: Heat cooking oil in a large pot or Dutch oven over medium-high heat. Brown the beef roll on all sides until well-sealed. Remove and set aside.
  5. Sauté Aromatics and Deglaze: In the same pot, sauté minced garlic and chopped onions until fragrant. Pour in vinegar and allow it to evaporate for a minute. Then add soy sauce, tomato sauce, beef broth, bay leaves, and black peppercorns. Bring to a simmer.
  6. Braise Morcon: Carefully return the browned beef roll to the pot. Bring the sauce to a gentle boil, then reduce heat to low, cover, and simmer for 2-3 hours, or until the beef is very tender. Turn the morcon occasionally to ensure even cooking.
  7. Slice and Serve: Once cooked, remove the morcon from the pot and let it rest for 10-15 minutes before removing the twine/skewers. Slice into thick rounds. Reduce the sauce further if desired, then pour over the sliced morcon. Serve hot with steamed rice.
