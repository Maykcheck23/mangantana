Burong Tilapia History

Burong tilapia is a fermented dish deeply rooted in pre-colonial food preservation practices. Before refrigeration, Filipinos used fermentation techniques to extend the shelf life of fish and rice while developing complex, pungent flavors. Particularly popular in Pampanga, this dish combines salted rice and tilapia, allowing it to ferment for several days. The final result is a sour, slightly pungent delicacy often eaten with fresh mustard leaves or fried meat to balance its strong taste. While fermentation may be acquired taste, burong tilapia highlights how early Filipinos maximized food resources, ensuring sustainability and efficiency in preserving staple ingredients.

Burong Tilapia Recipe

  • 1 kg tilapia or dalag (mudfish), filleted and thoroughly cleaned
  • 6 cups of cooked rice, cooled
  • 6-7 tbsp rock salt
  • Onions
  • Onions
  • Ginger
  • Tomatoes
  • Cooking oil

Burong Tilapia Procedure

  1. Place fish fillets on a clean, dry colander with a plate underneath. Sprinkle each side with salt. Set aside.
  2. Mix cooked rice with the remaining salt
  3. Get a clean, sterilized jar and layer the rice and salted, drained fillets. Start with the rice to ensure the mixture is evenly packed with no air pockets. Repeat until you use up the ingredients
  4. Cover mouth of the jar with cling wrap, then tightly screw on the lid. Place in a cool, dark place for 5 to 8 days. Every 2 to 3 days, you can check each jar and push the mixture down so it is covered by the expelled liquid
