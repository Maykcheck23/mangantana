Kutsinta History

Kutsinta, derived from the Malay word "kuih," entered Filipino culinary culture during the Spanish colonial era. The delicate balance of ingredients, with its chewy texture and sweet taste, has made Kutsinta a constant presence in Filipino households and festive gatherings. An interesting historical note is that Kutsinta is often associated with local street vendors and small neighborhood shops. It's a common sight to find vendors peddling Kutsinta along with puto (rice cakes) and other Filipino delicacies. The word "Kutsinta" itself is said to have originated from the Filipino verb "kutitap," meaning to twinkle, which perfectly captures the visual appeal of these sweet treats.

Kutsinta Ingredients

  • All Purpose Flour
  • Rice Flour
  • Brown Sugar
  • Lye Water
  • Water
  • Annato Extract
  • Grated Coconut

Kutsinta Recipe

  • 1 cup all-purpose flour
  • 1 cup rice flour
  • 1 1/2 cups brown sugar
  • 1 teaspoon lye water (or food-grade lye water substitute*)
  • 1 1/2 cups water
  • 1/2 teaspoon annatto extract (optional, for color)
  • Grated coconut for serving

Kutsinta Procedure

  1. In a mixing bowl, combine all-purpose flour, rice flour, and brown sugar.
  2. Gradually add water while stirring to avoid lumps.
  3. Add lye water (or substitute) and mix thoroughly.
  4. Strain the mixture to achieve a smooth batter.
  5. Add annatto extract for a vibrant color (optional).
  6. Pour the mixture into small molds, about 2/3 full.
  7. Steam for about 20-25 minutes or until a toothpick inserted comes out clean.
  8. Allow the Kutsinta to cool before removing them from the molds.
  9. Serve with freshly grated coconut on top.

    10. Special tips

    If lye water is not available, mix 1/4 teaspoon baking soda with 1 teaspoon water as a substitute. Adjust the sweetness according to your preference by adding more or less brown sugar. Freshly grated coconut enhances the flavor, but desiccated coconut can be used as an alternative.
